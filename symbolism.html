Symbols

Welcome to our exploration of Mongolian symbols.

өлзий хээ - Eternal Knot

The өлзий хээ, also known as the 'Eternal Knot,' symbolizes eternal love and unity across all realms. Often found adorning fences and integrated into architectural designs, it serves as a profound representation of enduring bonds and interconnectedness.

Соёмбо - (Soyombo)

The Соёмбо, Mongolia's national symbol, embodies rich cultural heritage and profound symbolism. Its components convey deep meanings: Flame: Flourishing family. Three prongs: Mongolian prosperity – past, present, future. Sun and crescent: Mongolian origins. Triangles: Defense of freedom and independence. Upper triangle triumphs over internal foes; lower represents victory over external adversaries. Rectangles: Honesty, justice, meritocracy. Yin-yang: Gender unity, once fish symbolizing vigilance, wisdom. Vertical rectangles: Fortress walls, symbolizing unity's strength. The Соёмбо is prominently featured on the Mongolian flag, as well as on government buildings and public spaces, serving as a powerful symbol of national identity and resilience.

хас тэмдэ - (khas temdeg)

The хас тэмдэ (khas temdeg), also known as the swastika, holds profound significance in Mongolian culture, dating back thousands of years. Revered as a symbol of good luck, prosperity, and auspiciousness, it is commonly displayed as a symbol of good fortune. You will commonly see this symbol on fences, patterns on clothing, and integrated in various artwork, embodying enduring cultural heritage and spiritual resonance.

алхан хээ

The symbol you're referring to, "алхан хээ" (pronounced "alkhan khee"), represents a hammer pattern commonly found in Mongolian traditional art, fences, walls, and various patterns. This symbol holds cultural significance in Mongolia and is deeply rooted in its history and heritage. The hammer pattern symbolizes strength, endurance, and resilience. In Mongolian culture, the hammer is associated with craftsmanship, as well as with the tools used by nomadic herders and craftsmen for various tasks such as building, crafting, and forging. It also symbolizes protection and defense, as sturdy fences and walls made using hammers were essential for safeguarding livestock and property in the nomadic lifestyle of the Mongolian people. The hammer pattern can be found in various forms of traditional Mongolian art, including woodwork, metalwork, and textiles. It is often incorporated into decorative motifs, such as on clothing, household items, and ceremonial objects. In addition to its aesthetic appeal, the hammer pattern carries deeper cultural and symbolic meanings, reflecting the values and traditions of Mongolian society. It serves as a reminder of the importance of resilience, hard work, and unity in overcoming challenges and building a strong and prosperous community.

Wheel of Dharma (Хүрд)

The Mongolian "Wheel of Dharma," known as "Хүрд" (pronounced "Hurd"), holds significant cultural and religious significance in Mongolian Buddhism. It represents one of the central symbols of Buddhist teachings and philosophy, embodying the concepts of spiritual enlightenment, moral guidance, and the path to liberation.

In Buddhism, the Wheel of Dharma, also known as the "Dharmachakra," symbolizes the teachings of the Buddha, specifically the Four Noble Truths and the Noble Eightfold Path. The Wheel is often depicted as a wheel with eight or more spokes, representing the eightfold path that leads to the cessation of suffering and the attainment of enlightenment.

The spokes of the Wheel symbolize various aspects of Buddhist practice and wisdom, including:

  • Right View: Understanding the true nature of reality and the causes of suffering.
  • Right Intention: Cultivating wholesome intentions and aspirations.
  • Right Speech: Practicing truthful, kind, and harmonious speech.
  • Right Action: Engaging in ethical conduct and avoiding harmful actions.
  • Right Livelihood: Choosing a livelihood that is honorable and conducive to spiritual growth.
  • Right Effort: Making diligent effort to cultivate positive qualities and overcome negative tendencies.
  • Right Mindfulness: Cultivating awareness and presence in the present moment.
  • Right Concentration: Developing focused and undistracted meditation to attain higher states of consciousness.
The Wheel of Dharma symbolizes the cyclical nature of existence and the perpetual journey of spiritual growth and realization. It represents the Buddha's teachings as a guiding force that leads individuals toward liberation from the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara).

Overall, the Wheel of Dharma holds profound spiritual significance in Mongolian culture, serving as a visual representation of the path to enlightenment and the enduring wisdom of Buddhist teachings.

National Emblem of Mongolia

The National Emblem of Mongolia (Mongolian: Монгол Улсын төрийн сүлд, Mongol Ulsīn törín süld) serves as the symbol of state for the government of Mongolia. It is prominently used on official documents such as Mongolian passports, government papers, and embassy placards.

Description:

  • Adoption: The state emblem was officially adopted on February 12, 1992, coinciding with the enforcement of the new constitution. Its design and specifications are outlined in Chapter 1, Article 12(3) of the Constitution of Mongolia.
  • Design Elements:
    • Outer Rim: The outer rim features a tumen nasan, symbolizing eternity, encircling a circular blue field that represents the sky.
    • Central Emblem: In the center of the blue field lies a combination of the Soyombo symbol and the wind horse (treasured steed), symbolizing Mongolia's independence, sovereignty, and spirit. These symbols draw inspiration from Mongolian heritage.
    • Sun, Moon, and Fire Symbols: Derived from the Xiongnu culture, these symbols are incorporated within the central emblem.
    • Cintamani: Positioned above the central emblem, the Cintamani (Чандмань) represents the Buddhist Three Jewels and is believed in Mongolian folklore to grant wishes.
    • Mountain Range: Below the central emblem, there's a depiction of a green mountain range with the Wheel of Dharma (Хүрд) at its center.
    • Khadag: At the bottom of the mountain range and wheel, a khadag (Хадаг), a ceremonial scarf, is depicted.

төрийн сулд - Wind Horse

In Mongolian culture, "төрийн сулд" (pronounced "törin süld") refers to the "Wind Horse." The Wind Horse is a powerful and widely recognized symbol that holds significant spiritual and cultural significance in Mongolian Buddhism and traditional beliefs.

Symbolism: The Wind Horse symbolizes vitality, energy, and good fortune. It is often depicted as a horse with billowing, wind-blown mane and tail, symbolizing the swift and dynamic force of the wind. The horse is typically depicted standing on a rocky mountain peak or flying through the sky, representing its ability to transcend earthly limitations and reach spiritual heights.

Spiritual Meaning: In Mongolian Buddhism, the Wind Horse is associated with positive energy and spiritual blessings. It is believed to carry prayers and aspirations to the heavens, acting as a messenger between the earthly realm and the divine. As such, the Wind Horse is often invoked in rituals and ceremonies to bring good luck, prosperity, and protection.

Cultural Significance: The Wind Horse is deeply ingrained in Mongolian culture and folklore. It is commonly depicted in traditional art, textiles, and decorative motifs, as well as in religious paintings and sculptures found in temples and monasteries. The symbol is also prevalent in Mongolian nomadic life, where horses have historically been revered as symbols of strength, freedom, and spiritual connection.

Attributes: The Wind Horse is often depicted with specific attributes that further enhance its symbolism. These may include auspicious symbols such as the flaming jewel or wish-fulfilling gem, representing enlightenment and fulfillment of desires. Additionally, the Wind Horse may be depicted alongside other symbolic elements such as the sun, moon, and stars, symbolizing cosmic harmony and the interconnectedness of all things.

Invocation: In Tibetan Buddhist practice, rituals involving the Wind Horse are performed to invoke its blessings and harness its positive energy. These rituals may include offerings, prayers, and visualization practices aimed at invoking the power of the Wind Horse to overcome obstacles, bring success, and fulfill wishes.

Overall, the Wind Horse holds a special place in Mongolian culture and spirituality, representing the innate desire for growth, prosperity, and spiritual fulfillment. It serves as a potent symbol of hope, inspiration, and connection to the divine in both religious and secular contexts.

Mens Ring

The "Bracelet of Khan," also known as "Khan-buguivch" in Mongolian, carries significant symbolic meaning in Mongolian culture, often representing peace, unity, and diplomacy. Historically, the Khan-buguivch was a special bracelet worn by Mongol khans, who were the rulers of various Mongol tribes and later established the Mongol Empire under leaders like Genghis Khan and his successors. These bracelets were typically crafted from precious metals such as gold or silver and adorned with intricate designs and sometimes gemstones, reflecting the wealth and power of the wearer. Symbolically, the Khan-buguivch served as a symbol of peace and diplomacy, especially during times of negotiation and alliance-building. When a khan presented his Khan-buguivch to another leader or emissary, it was often seen as a gesture of goodwill and a sign of the khan's commitment to maintaining peaceful relations and fostering alliances. The exchange of the Khan-buguivch was not only a diplomatic gesture but also a symbolic act of trust and mutual respect between parties. By accepting the bracelet, the recipient acknowledged the khan's authority and extended their own commitment to peace and cooperation. Throughout Mongolian history, the Khan-buguivch played a crucial role in diplomatic relations, helping to forge alliances and maintain stability within the region. It symbolized the importance of diplomacy and negotiation in resolving conflicts and promoting harmony among different tribes and nations. Even today, the symbolism of the Khan-buguivch continues to resonate in Mongolian culture, representing the values of peace, unity, and cooperation. While the tradition of exchanging Khan-buguivch may no longer be widespread, its legacy endures as a reminder of the importance of diplomacy and mutual respect in building a more peaceful world.

Womens Ring

The "Earrings of the Queen," also known as "Khatan-suikh" in Mongolian, hold significant cultural and symbolic meaning in Mongolian tradition. These earrings are deeply rooted in the history and folklore of Mongolia and are considered symbols of love, honesty, and marital fidelity. In Mongolian culture, earrings have long been associated with marital status and are often worn as symbols of love and commitment within marriage. The "Earrings of the Queen" specifically refer to a pair of earrings traditionally worn by Mongolian queens or prominent female figures, symbolizing their role as guardians of honesty and virtue within society. The earrings are typically adorned with precious metals and gemstones, reflecting the wearer's status and significance. They may feature intricate designs and craftsmanship, showcasing the wealth and prestige of the individual wearing them. Symbolically, the "Earrings of the Queen" represent not only the love and fidelity shared between spouses but also the broader ideals of honesty, integrity, and moral virtue. They serve as a reminder of the importance of trust and loyalty in relationships, as well as the responsibilities that come with positions of power and influence. In Mongolian folklore and tradition, stories and legends often highlight the significance of the "Earrings of the Queen" as talismans of love and protection. They are believed to bring blessings and good fortune to those who wear them, while also serving as symbols of the enduring bond between partners. Overall, the "Earrings of the Queen" hold a special place in Mongolian culture, representing not only the beauty and elegance of the wearer but also the values of love, honesty, and fidelity that are cherished within society.

Ger Door Pattern

The patterns on a ger door hold significant cultural and symbolic meaning in Mongolian tradition. The ger, also known as a yurt, is a portable dwelling used by nomadic peoples in Central Asia, including Mongolia. The door of the ger is a central feature, both functionally and symbolically, and often adorned with intricate patterns that carry deep cultural significance.

1. Geometric Patterns: Geometric patterns are prevalent on ger doors, representing harmony, balance, and unity. These patterns may include geometric shapes such as diamonds, squares, triangles, and circles, arranged in symmetrical and repetitive designs. The symmetry of these patterns symbolizes order and balance in the universe and reflects the nomadic lifestyle's connection to nature and the cosmos.

2. Animal Motifs: Animal motifs are frequently incorporated into ger door patterns, drawing inspiration from the rich biodiversity of the Mongolian steppe. Common animal motifs include horses, camels, deer, and mythical creatures such as dragons and phoenixes. These animal motifs symbolize strength, resilience, and the interconnectedness of humans and nature in the nomadic way of life.

3. Floral Designs: Floral designs are another prominent feature of ger door patterns, reflecting the beauty and abundance of the natural world. These designs may include flowers, leaves, vines, and other plant motifs, symbolizing growth, renewal, and vitality. Floral patterns also evoke feelings of warmth, hospitality, and welcome, reflecting the ger's role as a home and gathering place for family and community.

4. Symbolic Elements: Ger door patterns often incorporate symbolic elements drawn from Mongolian folklore, mythology, and spiritual beliefs. These may include auspicious symbols such as the eternal knot, representing the interconnectedness of all things, or the endless knot, symbolizing infinity and the cyclical nature of existence. Other symbols, such as the sun, moon, and stars, may also be featured, representing celestial forces and cosmic harmony.

5. Color Symbolism: The colors used in ger door patterns hold symbolic significance in Mongolian culture. Traditional colors such as blue, white, red, and yellow are commonly used, each carrying its own meaning. Blue symbolizes the sky and eternity, white represents purity and wisdom, red symbolizes strength and vitality, and yellow represents prosperity and abundance. These colors are carefully chosen to convey specific messages and evoke particular emotions.

Overall, the patterns on a ger door serve as a visual expression of Mongolian culture, history, and identity. They embody the values, beliefs, and traditions of the nomadic way of life, while also beautifying the ger and creating a welcoming and sacred space for its inhabitants.
